UTC to Eastern / Pacific: A Quick Conversion Cheat Sheet (Plus DST Notes)

Direct answer: UTC conversion depends on whether Eastern/Pacific are currently observing Standard Time or Daylight Time. During DST transition weeks, always double-check with a converter.

Because offsets change seasonally, this page focuses on a safe method plus a few common reference points.

The safe method (always correct)

Direct answer: Check whether the target zone is currently on Standard or Daylight time, then apply the correct offset.

  • Eastern: UTC-5 (Standard), UTC-4 (Daylight)
  • Pacific: UTC-8 (Standard), UTC-7 (Daylight)

Common reference conversions (examples)

Direct answer: Use these as quick sanity checks, not as your only source during DST weeks.

  • 12:00 UTC → morning in US (ET/PT)
  • 18:00 UTC → late morning/early afternoon in US (ET/PT)

(For exact daily times, use a converter—offsets depend on the date.)

DST: the trap you can avoid

Direct answer: The US and other regions don’t always change clocks on the same dates, so offsets can be “weird” for a couple weeks.

Rule:

  • In DST transition weeks, include the time zone label explicitly (“ET” or “PT”) and confirm with a converter.

A practical workflow

Direct answer: Put conversion where you schedule—not where you have to remember to look.

  • Secondary time zone in Google Calendar
  • A visible “what time is it in X?” tool
  • Meeting alerts as backup

FAQ

Why is UTC to Eastern sometimes 4 hours and sometimes 5?

Because Eastern switches between Daylight (UTC-4) and Standard (UTC-5).

Is there a permanent UTC cheat sheet?

Not a perfect one—DST makes “permanent” tables misleading. Use a method + a converter.
