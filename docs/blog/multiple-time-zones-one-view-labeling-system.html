Multiple Time Zones in One View: A Labeling System Your Team Will Actually Use

Direct answer: To track multiple time zones reliably, choose 2–4 team time zones, assign each a short label (SF/NY/LON), and keep them visible in your calendar or toolbar according to Google’s core documentation. Consistency beats cleverness.

This article gives you a system that reduces mistakes and makes scheduling faster.

Step 1: Choose your “team zones” (2–4 max)

Direct answer: More than 4 becomes noise.

Common sets:

  • SF / NY / LON
  • PT / ET / GMT
  • PT / ET / CET / IST

Step 2: Use labels people recognize instantly

Direct answer: Use city labels (SF/NY/LON) or unambiguous abbreviations.

Avoid ambiguous abbreviations:

  • “IST” can mean more than one region depending on context

Best practice:

  • City label + offset when needed (e.g., “LON (UTC+0)”)

Step 3: Decide a meeting-time convention

Direct answer: Pick one convention so nobody guesses.

Options:

  • Organizer’s time zone
  • Meeting location time zone
  • UTC for technical teams

Step 4: Make time zones visible where decisions happen

Direct answer: If time zones are hidden, people revert to mental math.

Good places:

  • Google Calendar secondary time zone
  • A toolbar time zone shortcut
  • A world clock extension

Step 5: Add a DST safety rule

Direct answer: DST transition weeks are where most “one-hour late” stories come from.

Rule:

  • During DST weeks, always include two zones in messages (“10am PT / 1pm ET”).

FAQ

How do I track more than 4 time zones?

Split them by team/project and only display the set you’re actively scheduling with.

Should we schedule in UTC?

UTC is great for distributed engineering teams, but many teams do better with labeled local time zones.
