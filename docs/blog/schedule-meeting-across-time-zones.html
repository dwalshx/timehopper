How to Schedule a Meeting Across Time Zones

Scheduling a meeting across New York, London, and Sydney means finding a window where three sets of working hours overlap. Most people solve this with mental math. Most people get it wrong at least once a quarter. This guide covers three tiers of tools, a comparison table, the DST traps that break recurring meetings, and the decision frameworks that generic tip lists skip.

Find the Overlap Window First

Start with working hours, not clocks. Convert each participant’s available range to a shared reference (UTC works well), then look for where the ranges intersect.

Here is a practical example. You need a 30-minute call with colleagues in New York (UTC-5), London (UTC+0), and Sydney (UTC+11):

  • New York working hours: 9 AM–5 PM ET = 14:00–22:00 UTC
  • London working hours: 9 AM–5 PM GMT = 09:00–17:00 UTC
  • Sydney working hours: 9 AM–5 PM AEDT = 22:00–06:00 UTC (next day)

The overlap is thin. 22:00 UTC is the only hour that falls within all three ranges: 5 PM in New York, 10 PM in London, 9 AM the next day in Sydney. London gets the worst slot. That is the reality of a 16-hour spread.

When the spread is too wide for a single window, you have two choices: rotate who takes the inconvenient time or go async. Both are covered below.

Use Google Calendar’s Built-In Time Zone Tools

Google Calendar lets you display a secondary time zone directly on your calendar grid. This is the fastest method when you only need two zones.

To set it up: open Settings > Time zone and enable Display secondary time zone. Pick the second zone from the dropdown. Your calendar grid now shows two time columns on the left side.

For additional cities, enable the World Clock sidebar under Settings > World Clock > Show world clock. This displays current times for any cities you add.

The limitation is real. The calendar grid shows exactly two time zones. The World Clock sidebar shows more, but it is display-only. You cannot click a sidebar time to create an event. For scheduling across three or more zones, you need a different tool.

Use a Standalone Time Zone Converter

When you need to compare three or more time zones at once, standalone converters show overlap at a glance.

timeanddate.com Meeting Planner lets you input multiple cities and see a color-coded grid. Green means normal working hours. Yellow and red flag early mornings and late nights. It handles 5,000+ locations, accounts for DST automatically, and is free. The limitation: it lives in a separate browser tab. You find the overlap, then manually create the calendar event.

World Time Buddy takes a visual approach with a slider interface. Drag across hours to see corresponding times in all selected zones. You can share a link to a configured meeting page, which is useful when proposing times to a group. It runs as a web app and a mobile app. Free tier available; premium removes ads.

Both tools are reliable. Both require you to leave your calendar, find the time, and come back to create the invite. For a deeper comparison of converter tools, see our time zone converter guide for meetings.

Use a Calendar-Integrated Time Zone Tool

Calendar-integrated tools solve the context-switching problem. Instead of leaving Google Calendar to check a converter, you see time zone information where you actually schedule.

TimeHopper is a Chrome extension that shows local times for other time zones directly on your Google Calendar grid. You add the zones you care about, and their times appear alongside your own. It handles three or more zones without the two-zone grid limitation that Google Calendar’s built-in feature has. When you click a time slot to create an event, you can see what that time means for every participant’s zone.

This is particularly useful for people who schedule cross-timezone meetings multiple times per week. The time zone context stays in your calendar rather than living in a separate tab.

Watch for DST Gaps

Daylight Saving Time starts and ends on different dates in different countries. A recurring meeting that works in February can silently shift by an hour in March without anyone changing the invite.

Here are the 2026 DST transition dates for the three most common scheduling regions:

RegionSpring ForwardFall Back
United StatesSunday, March 8Sunday, November 1
EU / UKSunday, March 29Sunday, October 25
Australia (observing states)Sunday, October 4Sunday, April 5

The critical gap: the US and EU spring-forward dates are three weeks apart. From March 8 to March 29, 2026, the US has already sprung forward but Europe has not. The US Eastern-to-London offset drops from 5 hours to 4. A recurring meeting set for “3:00 PM ET / 8:00 PM London” suddenly lands at 7:00 PM London time instead. No one changed the invite. The clocks did.

The same pattern repeats in the fall. The EU falls back on October 25, but the US does not fall back until November 1. For that week, the offset shifts again.

Australia adds a third layer. Its DST transitions happen in April and October, offset from both the US and EU schedules. A team spanning New York, London, and Sydney faces three separate transition windows per year where the usual time math breaks.

How to protect yourself:

  1. Use DST-aware tools (all four tools in the comparison table handle this).
  2. Audit your recurring meeting invites twice a year: once in March and once in October.
  3. Include explicit time zones in every invite. Write “3:00 PM ET / 8:00 PM GMT,” not just “3:00 PM.”

Decide When to Meet — and When to Go Async

Not every cross-timezone coordination needs a synchronous meeting. Harvard Business School research found that even a one-hour time zone gap reduces real-time communication by 11%. For routine coordination, teams shifted to async channels with no measurable productivity loss. The cost of synchronous meetings rises with the time zone spread.

Use these decision rules:

Schedule a synchronous meeting when:

  1. The topic requires real-time debate or complex decision-making
  2. The purpose is relationship building or team cohesion
  3. The issue is urgent and time-sensitive
  4. Misalignment risk is high and written communication has already failed

Go async when:

  1. The content is a status update or progress report
  2. The task is document review or feedback
  3. The handoff is routine and well-documented
  4. Participants span more than 8 hours of time zone difference and no overlap window exists within reasonable working hours

Doist, the company behind Todoist and Twist, operates across 35+ countries and treats async communication as the default. They reserve synchronous meetings for the scenarios listed above. If a fully distributed company spanning every continent can default to async, most teams can too.

The question is not “how do we schedule this meeting?” It is “does this need to be a meeting at all?”

Meeting Time Etiquette: Who Takes the Inconvenient Slot?

If one region always takes the early-morning or late-night call, resentment builds. Fairness matters for long-term team health.

Rotate meeting times. Set a cadence — monthly or quarterly — and shift the meeting time so the inconvenience moves between regions. Document the rotation schedule so everyone knows when their turn comes. Predictability matters more than perfection.

State time zones explicitly. Every meeting invite should include the time in each participant’s zone. Write “Tuesday 3:00 PM ET / 8:00 PM GMT / Wednesday 7:00 AM AEDT.” Calendar tools handle conversion, but the invite text should be readable without one.

Designate overlap windows. For teams that meet regularly, identify a standing overlap window rather than negotiating times ad hoc. Even a one-hour daily window reduces scheduling friction significantly.

Respect boundaries. A 7 AM call is manageable. A 6 AM call is a favor. A recurring 5 AM call is a retention problem. Know where the line is for each region.

Cross-timezone scheduling gets easier with three things: a tool that handles the conversion reliably, a framework for deciding what needs a live meeting, and a rotation system that distributes the inconvenience fairly.
