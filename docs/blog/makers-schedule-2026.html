The Maker’s Schedule in 2026: Was Paul Graham Right?

It is 9:04 on a Wednesday morning. A product manager in Denver has a three-hour block on her calendar. No meetings, no standups, just the spec she has been putting off since Monday. By 10:17, she has not written a word.

Here is what happened instead. At 9:11, a Slack thread from the London team needed a decision only she could make. At 9:28, she remembered a Loom review from yesterday she had not watched, and one of the comments tagged her directly. At 9:42, a calendar invite appeared for an 11am “quick align” with the Singapore lead. She accepted it without thinking, because saying no to a cross-timezone meeting that someone spent ten minutes scheduling feels like an act of aggression. At 10:03, she opened the spec document, reread the last paragraph she had written on Monday, and then checked Slack to see if the London thread had resolved. It had not.

Her deep-work block is technically intact. Nothing is scheduled until 11. But the time is already shredded, scattered into fragments too small for the cognitive work the spec requires, too numerous to feel like rest. Brigid Schulte has a term for this: time confetti. That product manager’s morning is full of it.

Seventeen years ago, Paul Graham described this problem in roughly 1,100 words. He called it “Maker’s Schedule, Manager’s Schedule”, and it became one of the most cited essays in the history of technology culture. Graham argued that makers — programmers, writers, anyone doing creative work — operate on a different time granularity than managers. Managers work in one-hour blocks. Makers need half-day stretches. A single meeting can ruin an entire afternoon.

He was right about almost everything. The world just changed underneath his framework.

What broke is every assumption surrounding that insight.

Graham’s core insight holds: makers need uninterrupted blocks, and meetings impose asymmetric costs on the people who build things. What broke is every assumption surrounding that insight. The co-located office. The clean separation between maker and manager. The idea that you control your own schedule. The real problem in 2026 is not the meeting on your calendar. It is everything around the meeting: the coordination tax that distributed work imposes on every hour of every day, whether you have a meeting or not.

The Real Argument Was About Time Granularity, Not Meetings

The most common reading of Graham’s essay reduces it to: meetings are bad for programmers, so don’t schedule meetings during their deep-work time. Useful simplification. Also a misreading that has pointed an entire generation of knowledge workers toward the wrong solution.

Graham’s actual argument was more precise. He observed that makers and managers operate on fundamentally different time granularities. A manager’s day is naturally divided into one-hour blocks, each slot interchangeable, each meeting a unit of work. A maker’s day cannot be subdivided that way. Creative and technical work requires sustained attention across half-day stretches. When you interrupt that stretch with a meeting, you do not just lose the hour. You lose the blocks on either side of it, because the maker either cannot start ambitious work knowing an interruption is coming, or cannot regain momentum after the interruption ends.

Graham quoted the observation that “the mere consciousness of an engagement will sometimes worry a whole day” to name the anticipation effect: the meeting at 2pm does not just consume the hour from 2 to 3. It suppresses productive work from lunch onward, because the maker knows the interruption is coming and cannot commit to anything that requires deep engagement.

This insight remains true. It may be more true now than when Graham wrote it. But notice what Graham was actually diagnosing. Not meetings per se, but the incompatibility between two modes of working and the time structures they require. The meeting was the most visible symptom in 2009. The underlying disease — the structural mismatch between how creative work happens and how organizations schedule time — has mutated since then.

Most Knowledge Workers Are Now Both Maker and Manager

The maker-manager binary has collapsed. In 2009, the programmer programmed and the manager managed. The boundaries were messy in practice, but the categories were legible. You could identify which schedule you were on and advocate for the one you needed.

In 2026, most knowledge workers toggle between both roles, sometimes in the same hour.

Consider that Denver product manager again. From 9 to noon, she needs to be a maker: writing a technical specification that requires sustained analytical thought, domain knowledge, and the kind of sentence-level precision that falls apart when attention is fragmented. From 1 to 2:30, she is a manager: running a sprint review, making prioritization calls, resolving a disagreement between engineering and design. From 2:30 to 4, she is a maker again: revising the spec based on what she heard in the review. From 4 to 5, she is a manager: async stand-up notes, Slack triage, a fifteen-minute one-on-one with a direct report.

This is the day. Every day. Microsoft’s 2025 Work Trend Index found that 52% of leaders and 48% of employees describe their work as “chaotic,” and the chaos is not a temporary condition. It is the structure. Reference: Microsoft 2025 Work Trend Index

I call this the Hybrid Trap. You must perform both maker and manager work within the same day, often the same morning, with no clean transition between modes. The trap is not that you have meetings. The trap is that you cannot be one thing.

Graham’s solution — put meetings at the end of the day — assumed you could draw a line between your maker hours and your manager hours. The Hybrid Trap means that line does not exist for most people. The maker’s schedule and the manager’s schedule are not two schedules competing for the same calendar. They are the same schedule, interleaved, fighting for the same hour.

Two heuristics make the Hybrid Trap survivable. First, the break-the-block threshold: only interrupt a maker block for something that is both high-severity and time-sensitive within the next four hours. A London thread that needs your input by end of their workday clears the threshold. A Loom review with a tomorrow deadline does not. If it fails either test, it waits. Second, the transition buffer: when switching from maker mode to manager mode, build in a ten-minute gap. Not for rest. For intentional context closure. Write one sentence capturing where you left off and what comes next. That sentence is what lets you re-enter the maker block at 2:30 instead of spending twenty minutes remembering where you were.

This is why time is not a resource you allocate — it is a coordination medium shaped by every person, team, and time zone your work touches. Graham treated time as something individual. The Hybrid Trap reveals it as something relational.

Time Confetti Is the Real Adversary

A single meeting ruining an afternoon was the 2009 version of the problem. The 2026 version is worse. The afternoon is not ruined by a meeting. It is shredded by hundreds of micro-interruptions, most of which never appear on a calendar.

Brigid Schulte coined the term “time confetti” in her 2014 book Overwhelmed to describe what happens when a schedule is fragmented into slivers — “one big, chaotic burst of exploding slivers, bits, and scraps” — too small for meaningful work, too numerous to feel like rest. She extended the argument in Over Work (2024), documenting how work has followed employees home: 81% check email outside work hours, 63% on weekends, 34% on vacation. Reference: Buffer State of Remote Work

The data from Microsoft’s 2025 Work Trend Index makes the confetti visible at scale. During core working hours, the average knowledge worker is interrupted every two minutes. That is approximately 275 interruptions per day across meetings, emails, and notifications. They receive 117 emails and 153 Teams messages daily. Fifty-seven percent of meetings are unscheduled, with no calendar invite. One in ten scheduled meetings is booked at the last minute. Reference: Microsoft 2025 Work Trend Index

Every two minutes. Graham worried about one meeting ruining an afternoon.

Every two minutes. Graham argued that a single meeting could ruin an afternoon. The modern knowledge worker faces an ambient field of interruption so dense that the concept of “an uninterrupted block” becomes almost theoretical.

Gloria Mark, a professor of informatics at UC Irvine, has tracked this erosion longitudinally. In 2004, workers maintained focus on a screen for about two and a half minutes before switching tasks. By 2012, that dropped to 75 seconds. Her most recent measurement: 47 seconds. Forty-seven. And the cost of context switching has not changed. It still takes an average of 25 minutes to return full attention to a task after an interruption. Reference: Gloria Mark, Attention Span (2023)

Here is the part that would have surprised Graham: workers are now as likely to interrupt themselves as to be interrupted by others. When external interruptions decline, internal interruptions increase. The habit of fragmentation becomes internalized. Remove the meetings and the Slack pings, and many knowledge workers will still reach for their phone, check email, open a new tab. The confetti is not just environmental. It has become cognitive.

You know this feeling. You clear your calendar, close Slack, put on headphones, and fifteen minutes later you are checking your inbox anyway. Not because anything happened. Because the interruption has been trained into you by years of operating in confetti time. The reflex outlasts the stimulus. Graham assumed that if you remove the meeting, the maker gets the block. Mark’s research suggests the damage goes deeper.

The Coordination Tax Nobody Budgeted For

She has three browser tabs open: Google Calendar, World Time Buddy, Slack. The cursor blinks in a calendar search bar. She is trying to find 30 minutes that work for San Francisco, London, and Singapore. Every slot she picks violates someone’s maker block or lands at 10pm local. The meeting is not the cost. The fifteen minutes she just spent failing to schedule it is.

I call it the coordination tax: the cumulative time and cognitive overhead of synchronizing work across distributed teams, time zones, and communication tools. The Slack thread confirming the agenda. The three people who need to watch a Loom before the meeting can be productive, only one of whom has watched it. The follow-up message translating the meeting’s decisions into action items because two attendees joined from a time zone where it was 10pm and they were not fully present. The calendar invite that says “30 minutes” but requires 45 minutes of overhead on either side.

Graham wrote his essay at Y Combinator, where founders and investors shared a building. His “office hours” solution — cluster all meetings at the end of the day — assumed physical proximity, a single time zone, and a small number of coordination partners. It was elegant for that context. But in 2026, Microsoft’s data shows that 30% of meetings span multiple time zones, up 8 percentage points since 2021. After-hours meetings have increased 16% year-over-year, because when your team is globally distributed, someone’s deep-work block is always someone else’s only available window. Reference: Microsoft 2025 Work Trend Index

The meeting problem has also become recursive. Atlassian’s 2024 research across 5,000 knowledge workers found that 77% of meetings end by scheduling another meeting. Think about that for a second. The primary output of most meetings is more meetings. Seventy-two percent are rated ineffective by the people who attend them. And yet 78% of workers report they cannot complete their work despite attending all their meetings, or perhaps because of it. Reference: Atlassian Workplace Woes: Meetings, 2024

The coordination tax falls hardest on the people Graham never wrote about. He wrote from the maker’s perspective: the programmer who wants to be left alone. But someone has to coordinate. The operations leads, executive assistants, and program managers who schedule across continents, reconcile conflicting calendars, and absorb the consequences when a time zone conversion goes wrong. They pay the coordination tax so that makers can have their blocks. Their work is invisible in Graham’s framework. Nobody writes essays about the executive assistant who spent 40 minutes finding a slot that does not ruin anyone’s Tuesday.

If scheduling a synchronous meeting requires more than fifteen minutes of calendar Tetris across three or more time zones, the default should be async, with a clear decision path for when synchronous conversation is genuinely necessary. This is a coordination principle, not a productivity tip: the cost of synchronization should be weighed against the cost of asynchronicity, and most teams never make that calculation explicitly.

Calendar Architecture, Not Calendar Hygiene

Individual calendar blocking cannot solve a structural problem. Graham’s essay spawned a generation of advice — “Protect your maker time,” “Block off deep-work hours,” “Say no to meetings” — that treats calendar health as personal hygiene. Not wrong. Insufficient.

The distinction that matters is between hygiene and architecture. Calendar hygiene is what one person does with their own calendar. Calendar architecture is the deliberate design of coordination systems, defaults, and team norms that make maker time structurally possible rather than individually defended.

Here is the difference. Calendar hygiene says: “Block 9am to noon for deep work.” Calendar architecture says: “The team’s default is no meetings before noon. Async updates are due by 10am. Synchronous time is reserved for decisions that require real-time dialogue, and those are batched into a two-hour window three afternoons per week. Exceptions require a reason.”

Hygiene depends on one person’s discipline. Architecture depends on shared agreements. Hygiene breaks the first time a senior leader sends a 9:30am meeting invite. Architecture bends but does not break, because the norms are explicit and the defaults are structural.

Hygiene breaks the first time a senior leader sends a 9:30am meeting invite.

Three structural elements make architecture real. First, a coordination protocol: define which communication types belong in which channels. Decisions go in a shared document with a 24-hour comment window. Status updates go in async standups. Only unresolved conflicts and time-sensitive decisions earn a synchronous meeting. Second, batched sync windows: instead of scattering meetings across the week, compress all synchronous time into predictable blocks — say, Tuesday and Thursday afternoons. Every other hour defaults to maker time. The predictability matters as much as the protection; the anticipation effect disappears when you know exactly when and where meetings live. Third, a quarterly audit cadence: every 90 days, review every recurring meeting against one question — what decision does this meeting make that cannot be made asynchronously? Rebecca Hinds and Robert Sutton documented this approach in Harvard Business Review: 60 employees evaluated 1,160 standing meetings, eliminated 30%, restructured 27%, and saved 265 hours per month. The intervention was not “block your calendar.” It was a structural audit of the coordination layer. Reference: Hinds & Sutton, “Meeting Overload Is a Fixable Problem,” HBR, 2022

Cal Newport saw the architecture distinction forming. His 2016 book Deep Work operationalized Graham’s insight as individual discipline, rules for focused work in a distracted world. His 2021 book A World Without Email shifted toward organizational-level solutions: structured communication protocols, scheduling systems that reduce ad-hoc messaging, the argument that protecting deep work is a competitive advantage rather than a personal preference. Newport was moving in the right direction. But even A World Without Email underestimated the coordination dimension, the specific overhead of distributed, cross-timezone work that requires more than better email protocols.

Before adding a recurring meeting to anyone’s calendar, answer one question: what decision does this meeting make that cannot be made asynchronously? If the answer is unclear, the meeting is coordination theater. It performs the appearance of collaboration without producing the outcome collaboration requires. Which is really an argument for meetings that actually accomplish something.

I want to be honest about the limits here. “Async by default, synchronous by exception” is a clean principle. Reality is messier. Async communication creates its own costs: ambiguity that a five-minute conversation would resolve, delayed decisions that block downstream work, the loss of social cohesion that comes from never seeing your colleagues in real time. Calendar architecture is a design challenge, not a solved problem, and most organizations are not even framing it as one.

If you suspect your calendar needs architectural work rather than another round of hygiene, a structured calendar audit is where the diagnosis begins.

The Maker’s Schedule as Infrastructure

Let me return to that product manager in Denver. Same Wednesday. Same spec. But imagine a different coordination layer around her.

Her team operates on an async-by-default protocol. The London thread from 9:11am still happens, but the team norm is that async decisions have a four-hour response window. Nobody expects an answer within minutes. The Loom review has a clear deadline attached, and it is tomorrow, not today. The 11am “quick align” does not happen, because the Singapore lead posted the question in a shared document and tagged the relevant people, and the answer materialized across time zones without requiring anyone to be in the same room at the same time.

Her 9-to-noon block survives because the system defends it: team norms, communication defaults, and coordination infrastructure that compress the synchronous dependency into smaller, more predictable windows. She still has meetings. They start at 1pm, they are batched, and they end with clear decisions. Her calendar shows London and Singapore time alongside her own, so when she does schedule across zones, the math is already done. No tab-switching, no second-guessing, no fifteen-minute scheduling tax. TimeHopper was built on exactly that premise: make the coordination layer visible and automatic so the maker’s block survives contact with a distributed team. The spec gets written.

This is what Graham was reaching for, even if the world he was writing in did not require it yet. The maker’s schedule was always an infrastructure argument disguised as a personal preference. Graham framed it as “please give me my time back.” The 2026 version is: “the systems we build to coordinate work determine whether deep work is possible.” One is a request. The other is a design problem.

Graham was right that makers need blocks. He was right that meetings impose asymmetric costs. He was right that the people who set the schedule often do not understand the cost they are imposing. What he could not have known — what nobody writing in 2009 could have anticipated — is that the meeting would stop being the primary threat. The coordination tax, the ambient interruption field, the hybrid trap of being both maker and manager in the same hour, the recursive meeting culture that generates its own demand. These are structural forces that individual calendar hygiene cannot defeat.

The maker’s schedule in 2026 is not a personal preference to be respected. It is an infrastructure problem to be solved. And infrastructure problems require architecture, not discipline.

None of this is fully solved. Most organizations will keep defaulting to meetings because meetings feel like progress. The coordination tax will keep rising as teams become more distributed. Calendar architecture is easier to describe than to build. But we have something Graham’s original readers did not: language for what is actually breaking, data on how it breaks, and a growing body of evidence that structural interventions work. Whether most organizations will use any of it is a different question.
