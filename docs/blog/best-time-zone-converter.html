Best Time Zone Converter in 2026: Tools and Apps Compared

Most “best time zone converter” roundups list a dozen tools with two-sentence descriptions and no evaluation criteria. That does not help you choose. This article rates eight tools against the same rubric, segments them by form factor, and ends with a decision matrix so you can pick the right one in under a minute.

All features and pricing verified as of March 2026.

What Makes a Good Time Zone Converter

Not every converter solves the same problem. A travel blogger checking sunrise times in Kathmandu has different needs than a project manager scheduling standups across four offices. The rubric below is what separates a genuinely useful converter from a toy.

The Evaluation Rubric

CriterionWhat it measuresWhy it matters
Accuracy / DST handlingCorrect offsets including DST transitions, half-hour zones (India +5:30, Nepal +5:45, Newfoundland -3:30), historical datesThe #1 reason conversions fail. A tool that ignores DST transition days will silently give you the wrong time.
Speed to resultClicks or seconds from intent to answerYou convert time zones dozens of times a week. Every extra click compounds.
Ad / clutter loadAds, interstitials, cookie banners that slow down a 10-second taskAd-heavy converters turn a quick lookup into a frustrating experience.
Calendar integrationWhether the tool connects to your calendar or requires manual transferConverting a time means nothing if you then have to type it into your calendar by hand.
Platform availabilityWeb, browser extension, mobile app, cross-browser supportA web-only tool is useless when you are scheduling from your phone on a train.
Team featuresShared views, meeting voting, contact time zone trackingSolo converters work for freelancers. Distributed teams need coordination tools.
PricingFree, freemium, or paid. Ad-supported or ad-free.Most converters are free. The few that charge need to justify it with features the free tools lack.

Every tool below is rated against these seven criteria. No exceptions.

Best Web-Based Time Zone Converters

Web converters are the most feature-rich category. They handle the widest range of use cases, from quick single-pair lookups to multi-city meeting planning. The trade-off: you have to leave whatever you are doing and open a browser tab.

timeanddate.com

The reference standard for time zone data. timeanddate.com has been online since 1998, and its converter handles edge cases that most tools ignore: historical DST data, half-hour and quarter-hour offsets, future DST transition dates, and conversions across up to 12 locations simultaneously.

The Meeting Planner is a separate tool that finds overlap windows across multiple cities with color-coded time blocks. It accounts for local holidays, not just working hours.

Strengths: Most comprehensive location database (5,000+). Handles India, Nepal, Newfoundland, and other non-standard offsets without errors. 15-minute increment slider plus exact minute entry. Export to Outlook, Thunderbird, iCal.

Limitations: Ad-heavy free tier. No browser extension. No calendar integration beyond manual export. Functional but dated interface. Single-user only.

Pricing: Free (ad-supported). Premium ad-free subscription available.

Best for: Complex conversions, historical dates, edge-case offsets, meeting planning with holiday awareness.

World Time Buddy

The best visual layout among web-based converters. WorldTimeBuddy displays multiple time zones on a single horizontal table. You drag to select a time range and see it mapped across all locations instantly. A Google Calendar overlay shows your existing events alongside the comparison, though it is read-only.

All features have been free since May 2022 when WorldTimeBuddy discontinued its paid plans. For more on how it compares to other options, see our WorldTimeBuddy alternatives roundup.

Strengths: Best-in-class visual comparison. Drag-and-drop meeting time selection. Google Calendar overlay. Completely free. AM/PM and 24-hour format. Event page creation for sharing proposed times.

Limitations: Web-first; mobile apps are less capable. No browser extension. Google Calendar overlay is read-only (cannot create events). No team accounts. Ad-supported.

Pricing: Free.

Best for: Visual multi-zone comparison, quick meeting scheduling for small groups.

Every Time Zone

Clean, minimal design with the lowest cognitive load of any web converter. The free trial gives you a horizontal slider to compare zones. The real value is in the paid Pro plan: invite coworkers, group contacts by team, and use meeting time voting to eliminate scheduling back-and-forth.

Strengths: Minimal visual design. Team features on Pro (contact tracking, groups, meeting voting). Unlimited contacts for sales leads and clients.

Limitations: No free tier for ongoing use. $39–119/year may not justify the cost for individuals when most competitors are free. No native mobile app. No browser extension. No calendar integration.

Pricing: Small plan $39/year ($3.25/month). Pro plan $119/year ($10/month). Free trial available. Pricing verified March 2026; subject to change.

Best for: Teams that need shared time zone tracking and meeting voting. Not cost-effective for solo users.

Best Browser Extensions for Time Zone Conversion

Browser extensions eliminate the tab-switch. Instead of navigating to a converter site, the conversion lives inside your browser workflow. This category is smaller than web tools but growing.

TimeHopper

Disclosure: TimeHopper is our product. It is evaluated here using the same rubric as every other tool.

TimeHopper overlays time zone conversion directly onto Google Calendar. World clocks and a converter are always visible in your calendar view. You never open a separate tab, navigate to a site, or mentally map a converted time back to your schedule.

Strengths: Calendar-integrated conversion. Always visible inside Google Calendar. Zero context switching. Lightweight Chrome extension with minimal permissions. Free.

Limitations: Chrome only. Requires Google Calendar (not useful for Outlook or other calendar platforms). No standalone mobile app. No meeting planner for large groups (10+ participants). No bulk or historical date conversion. Smaller user base than established tools.

Pricing: Free.

Best for: Chrome users who work in Google Calendar daily and convert time zones as part of scheduling. Not a fit for mobile-first workflows or bulk conversions.

Savvy Time

Savvy Time covers two form factors: a web converter and a Chrome extension. The extension’s standout feature is auto-detection. It scans web page text for times and converts them to your local zone in-place. Useful when reading emails, Slack messages, or event pages with times in other zones.

Strengths: Web + extension combo. Auto-detects and converts times on web pages. Compare up to 10 zones. Free with no account required.

Limitations: Extension is a popup converter, not integrated into any calendar. Auto-detection can misfire on ambiguous time formats. Web tool UI is less polished than WorldTimeBuddy. No team features. No mobile app.

Pricing: Free.

Best for: Users who encounter times in other zones while browsing and want instant in-page conversion.

FoxClocks

FoxClocks displays small world clocks in your browser status bar. It is always visible, covers virtually all time zones, and supports Chrome, Firefox, and Thunderbird. Extensive customization: per-clock colors, custom time formats, hour-based color changes.

Strengths: Always visible without clicking. Cross-browser (Chrome, Firefox, Thunderbird). Highly customizable. Privacy-respecting. Long-established with a loyal user base.

Limitations: World clock display only. Not a converter. You cannot input a future time and see the conversion. No calendar integration. UI feels dated. Status bar clocks can interfere with some page layouts.

Pricing: Free.

Best for: Passive awareness of current time across zones. Not suitable if you need to convert specific times or dates.

Best Mobile Time Zone Converter Apps

Mobile apps suit travelers and people who schedule outside desktop hours. The field is thinner than web, and mobile versions of web tools are typically less capable than their desktop counterparts.

timeanddate.com World Clock App

The most comprehensive mobile world clock available. Same trusted data as the web tools: 5,000+ locations, DST transition alerts, sunrise/sunset data. Set alarms for other time zones, which is genuinely useful for international calls. iCloud sync across devices. Works offline.

Pricing: Paid app (one-time purchase, price varies by platform).

Limitations: Information-dense UI. Separate apps for different functions (World Clock, Meeting Planner). No calendar integration beyond basic export.

World Time Buddy Mobile

Free mobile companion to the web tool. Same visual comparison layout. Available on both iOS and Android. The main drawback: the smaller screen reduces the visual advantage that makes the web version so effective. No Google Calendar overlay on mobile.

Pricing: Free.

Neither mobile app offers calendar-integrated conversion. If that workflow matters on mobile, the gap remains open.

The Case for Calendar-Integrated Conversion

Most time zone conversions happen because someone is scheduling a meeting. The workflow usually looks like this: leave your calendar, open a converter, enter cities, read the result, switch back to your calendar, create the event. That is five context switches for a 10-second task.

Calendar-integrated tools collapse this into one step. The conversion happens where the scheduling happens. This is not a niche preference. It is a workflow architecture question. Any tool that puts time zone context inside your calendar view reduces friction for the people who convert zones most often: daily schedulers.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which time zone converter handles DST best?

timeanddate.com has the most thorough DST handling, including historical data, future transitions, and non-standard offsets like India (+5:30) and Nepal (+5:45). WorldTimeBuddy and TimeHopper handle current DST transitions automatically but do not support historical date lookups.

Is there a free time zone converter with no ads?

WorldTimeBuddy is free with moderate ads. TimeHopper, Savvy Time, and FoxClocks are completely free with no ads. Every Time Zone is ad-free but requires a paid subscription after the trial. timeanddate.com’s free tier has significant ad clutter; its premium tier removes ads.

What’s the best time zone converter Chrome extension?

It depends on what you need. TimeHopper is best for calendar-integrated conversion inside Google Calendar. Savvy Time is best for auto-detecting times on web pages. FoxClocks is best for passive world clock display. All three are free.
