# TimeHopper Editorial Personas

**Purpose:** Stable reference personas for the editorial pipeline. These inform the Brief Architect (who is this article for?), the Writer (what scenarios to write toward), and the Critique Session (would this actually help these people?).

Derived from the TimeHopper Composable Persona System. Each persona below includes editorial-specific notes: what content they seek, how they read, what they skip, and what makes content valuable to them.

---

## 1. The Operator (High-Stakes Coordinator)

**Profile:** T3 / C3 / P1-P2 | Coordination + Risk Prevention | Reputation risk anxiety | Manual-Assisted control | High trust threshold | Continuous rhythm

**Who they are:** Manages a globally distributed team. Their calendar is a mission-critical coordination tool — a wrong time zone on a meeting invite means a client misses a kickoff or a team ships late. They've been burned before and now double-check everything.

**Examples:** Executive assistant to a C-suite leader, program manager for a global product launch, operations lead coordinating across APAC/EMEA/US.

### Editorial Notes

- **What they search for:** Specific, high-confidence answers to coordination problems. "How to set up recurring meetings across time zones." "Does Google Calendar adjust for DST automatically?"
- **How they read:** Scans for the direct answer, then reads edge cases and gotchas carefully. Trusts tables and decision rules over prose. Wants to know what can go wrong.
- **What they skip:** Generic introductions, obvious advice ("communicate with your team"), anything that feels like it hasn't been tested.
- **What makes content valuable:** Specificity about failure modes. Platform-specific caveats. "If you're on Outlook and your attendee is on Google Calendar, here's what actually happens." Decision rules they can apply immediately.
- **Tone they respond to:** Confident, precise, no hedging. They want an expert peer, not a tutorial.

---

## 2. The Planner (Time Architect)

**Profile:** T1-T2 / C2-C3 / P2 | Planning | Efficiency + overload anxiety | Assisted-Automated control | Medium trust threshold | Transition-Continuous rhythm

**Who they are:** Uses their calendar as a planning tool — time blocking, prep windows, buffer slots. They think in systems and workflows, not individual events. They're always looking for a better way to structure their week.

**Examples:** Product manager who time-blocks deep work, freelancer juggling multiple clients across 2-3 time zones, team lead who pre-plans the entire sprint's meetings.

### Editorial Notes

- **What they search for:** Workflow-level solutions, not just feature how-tos. "Best way to time-block across time zones." "How to set up a weekly planning template in Google Calendar."
- **How they read:** Reads thoroughly if the content promises a system or workflow. Loves numbered steps they can implement. Will bookmark and return.
- **What they skip:** Content that only explains what a feature does without showing how to use it strategically. Listicles without depth.
- **What makes content valuable:** Complete workflows they can adopt. "Here's a system for..." with concrete steps. Templates and examples of real setups. Before/after comparisons.
- **Tone they respond to:** Thoughtful, structured, slightly nerdy. They appreciate when someone has clearly thought about the problem systematically.

---

## 3. The Juggler (Overloaded Knowledge Worker)

**Profile:** T2 / C2 / P3 | Reference + Execution | Cognitive overload anxiety | Manual-Assisted control | Medium trust threshold | Transition rhythm

**Who they are:** Their calendar is their external brain — if it's not on the calendar, it doesn't exist. They're not trying to optimize; they're trying to survive. They have too many meetings, too many tools, and not enough time to figure out why something isn't working.

**Examples:** Mid-level manager with back-to-back meetings across 2 time zones, remote worker who just moved and everything is showing the wrong time, parent balancing work calendar with family logistics.

### Editorial Notes

- **What they search for:** Immediate fixes to things that just broke. "Google Calendar showing wrong time zone." "How to change time zone on Google Calendar." They search mid-crisis.
- **How they read:** Skims aggressively. Needs the answer in the first 2-3 sentences under the heading. Will leave immediately if the page doesn't look like it has their answer. Checklists and numbered steps are lifelines.
- **What they skip:** Everything that isn't directly solving their problem. Context, background, comparisons — all invisible to them in the moment.
- **What makes content valuable:** Speed to answer. Scannable structure. "Do this, then this, then this." Screenshots described clearly. A troubleshooting table they can ctrl+F.
- **Tone they respond to:** Calm, direct, no-nonsense. Like a helpful coworker who just tells you what to click.

---

## 4. The Minimalist (Lightweight Checker)

**Profile:** T0-T1 / C0-C1 / P1-P4 | Reference | Minor inefficiency anxiety | Manual control | Low trust threshold | Event-triggered rhythm

**Who they are:** Barely uses their calendar. When they do search for calendar help, it's because something specific and annoying happened — a notification didn't fire, a meeting showed up at the wrong time, they can't figure out how to do one specific thing. They don't want to become a power user; they want the problem to go away.

**Examples:** Solo worker who just needs meetings to show up on time, someone who got a new phone and the calendar isn't syncing, occasional traveler who noticed their calendar times are wrong.

### Editorial Notes

- **What they search for:** Single-task queries. "How to turn on Google Calendar notifications." "Why is my calendar one hour off." They search the exact symptom.
- **How they read:** Wants the shortest possible path to done. If the article looks long, they'll try a different result. A visible checklist or "Quick Fix" section near the top is the difference between staying and bouncing.
- **What they skip:** Anything that looks like it's for power users. Comparisons, workflow advice, system-level thinking — all irrelevant. They don't want to learn; they want to fix.
- **What makes content valuable:** A fast, correct answer with minimal surrounding content. A "Quick Fix" box at the top. Clear confirmation of "you're done" at the end.
- **Tone they respond to:** Simple, friendly, zero jargon. Like a help article that doesn't make them feel dumb.

---

## Using Personas in the Pipeline

### Brief Architect (Step 1)
Identify 1-2 primary personas for each article. Use their search behavior and reading style to shape the content strategy. Ask: "What is [Persona] searching for, and what do they need to see in the first 10 seconds?"

### Writer (Steps 3-4)
Write toward the primary persona's reading style. Use their scenarios as examples. Structure the content for how they scan. If writing for a Juggler, the answer must be immediate. If writing for a Planner, the system/workflow must be complete.

### Critique Session (Step 5)
The User Lens critic evaluates the draft from the primary persona's perspective: "Would the Juggler actually find their answer here? Would the Operator trust this enough to act on it?"

### Backlog Spinout
When a section is clearly serving a different persona than the article's primary audience, that's a spinout candidate. The Planner doesn't need the Minimalist's quick-fix, and vice versa — that's two articles.
